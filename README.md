# Predicting Monthly MRSA Hospital Infections Using a Compartmental SCIR Transmission Model
SCIR Model
The proposed SCIR model is governed by a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs), where transitions between compartments are determined by biological and epidemiological processes. The original model included both MRSA and MSSA infections, however I have adjusted the model slightly to account for only MRSA infections which are the focus of this paper. The model used is described by the following equations.

Equation 1 represents the susceptible (S) compartment of individuals who are not currently colonized or infected with MRSA but are at risk of acquiring the pathogen. These individuals may become colonized through contact with colonized (C) or infected (I) individuals, with transmission mediated by the parameters λ_C and λ_I, respectively. Alternatively, they may become directly infected through exposure to infectious individuals, governed by the infection rates β_C  and β_I. Susceptible individuals can also enter the population at a rate πN, representing hospital admissions or new entries into the environment, and may return to susceptibility from the recovered (R) compartment through loss of immunity at rate μ. They exit the susceptible pool either through infection or via general system loss, such as discharge or death, modeled by the rate δ. N is the total population present in all compartments.

Equation 2 represents the colonized compartment which includes individuals who have acquired MRSA but do not yet exhibit clinical symptoms of infection. Colonization can occur through contact with either colonized individuals at rate λ_C or infected individuals at rate λ_I. While colonized individuals are typically asymptomatic, they play a critical role in the persistence and transmission of MRSA within healthcare settings. From this state, individuals may either clear the colonization naturally at rate α, progress to active infection at rate γ, or exit the system entirely at rate δ. Colonized individuals are considered a key intermediary stage, serving both as a reservoir of transmission and a precursor to clinical infection.

Equation 3 represents the infectious compartment with individuals who have developed symptomatic MRSA infections. These cases arise from two primary pathways: progression from the colonized state at rate γ, and direct infection due to contact with other infectious individuals, at rates β_C  and β_I, depending on whether exposure was from colonized or infected individuals. Infectious individuals contribute to ongoing transmission within the hospital and are assumed to be under treatment. The recovered compartment represented by Equation 4 consists of individuals who have successfully recovered from MRSA infection and temporarily retain immunity to re-infection. This temporary protection is assumed to wane over time, with individuals returning to the susceptible state at rate μ. While in the recovered state, individuals are not considered infectious and do not contribute to ongoing transmission. However, similar to other compartments, they may leave the population at rate δ. This compartment captures both the benefit and limitations of post-infection immunity in the context of MRSA epidemiology.
The proposed SCIR model is based on key assumptions to ensure biological and epidemiological plausibility. Infected individuals are assumed to be more contagious than colonized individuals due to higher bacterial loads, making transmission more likely through contact with infected persons. Direct infection is less likely than colonization, and colonized individuals are at a higher risk of progressing to infection. The model assumes that recovery from MRSA infection provides only temporary immunity, reflected in the short-term recovered state before returning to susceptibility. Additionally, the model acknowledges that hospital-based surveillance captures only a portion of the total infections [5]. With these assumptions, the objective of this study is to evaluate the performance of the SCIR model in capturing MRSA dynamics in a hospital setting and estimate key transmission parameters using real-world surveillance data.
